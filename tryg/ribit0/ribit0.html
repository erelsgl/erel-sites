    Governments choose policies through complicated interactions between many different interest groups.  This process generates economically suboptimal choices in many contexts. Minimum wages constrain the ability of workers and firms to arrive at mutually beneficial contracts and thereby create needless unemployment.  There are large distortionary transfers made to farmers in virtually every developed country.

    Having a zero nominal interest rate is ECONOMICALLY optimal.  But there are apparently political reasons why central banks find it in their interest to deviate from this optimal prescription.  It is worth pointing out that most economic studies find the efficiency losses associated with low nominal interest rates (say,  two-three percent) to be quite small - almost certainly much smaller than the losses associated with high capital income taxes, bad trade policy, bad minimum wage policy, or bad farm policy.
